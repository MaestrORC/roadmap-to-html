Transfer Locations on Federal Probation

The same rules and processes for transferring and moving residences apply to both federal probation and Supervised Release (Supervised Release transfers discussed on PG.¬†240).

I am on federal probation or supervised release, and I want to move. How can I do that?

The process depends on whether you are asking to move to a new residence within the same district OR to a new residence in a different district.

In a nutshell, it‚Äôs easier to move within the same district than to move to a new one. The steps below outline both possibilities:

POSSIBILITY #1:

If you are moving to a new residence within your current district, follow these steps:

Notify your Probation Officer that you want to change your address, and submit that address and the contact information for anyone else living at that address. You must get permission from your Probation Officer to move within your current U.S. Probation District‚Äîeven if it‚Äôs across the street.Your Probation Officer will investigate the new address‚Äîso long as it is located in the same U.S. Probation District. As part of that investigation, your Probation Officer will:

Make sure the new address actually exists;

Make sure that other people living at the new address are willing and able to have you in their home;

Run a background check on everyone living at the new address;

PLEASE NOTE: Since it is a standard condition for all people on Federal Probation or Supervised Release to avoid associating with anyone else who has a felony conviction, your request to move/transfer to live with someone who has been convicted of a felony will likely be denied.

Make sure that everyone at the new address knows about and agrees to the ‚ÄúSearch Condition‚Äù of your Federal Probation or Supervised Release.[798]

Make sure there are no weapons at the new address.

POSSIBILITY #2:

If you are moving to a new residence outside of your current U.S. Probation District, it‚Äôs suggested that you follow these steps:

STEP 1: Before you ask for a formal transfer to a new district‚Äîwhich can be a longer, more challenging process‚Äîask your probation officer for what is called ‚Äúcourtesy supervision‚Äù by another district. This technically keeps your case in the original district, but allows you to live in and travel to the district of your choice. Your probation officer AND the probation officer of the other district have to agree. After doing this, it is much easier to transfer to the courtesy district than to just transfer from one district to another without ‚Äúcourtesy supervision‚Äù being set up first.

STEP 2: If ‚Äúcourtesy supervision‚Äù is denied or doesn‚Äôt work out, you can still request a formal transfer. Tell your Probation Officer that you want to change your address, and submit that address and the contact information for anyone else living at that address. You must get permission from your Probation Officer to move to a new address in a different U.S. Probation District.

STEP 3: Your Probation Officer must submit a ‚ÄúTransfer Investigation‚Äù to the new district. The Transfer Investigation generally takes 30 days or longer, since both your current district and the new district must investigate your new proposed address and approve the transfer. As part of the ‚ÄúTransfer Investigation,‚Äù a Probation Officer in the receiving District will:

Make sure the new address actually exists;

Make sure that other people living at the new address are willing and able to have you in their home;

Run a background check on everyone living at the new address;

PLEASE NOTE: Since it is a standard condition for all people on Federal Probation or Supervised Release to avoid associating with anyone else who has a felony conviction, your request to move/transfer to live with someone who has been convicted of a felony will likely be denied.

Make sure that everyone at the new address knows about and agrees to the ‚ÄúSearch Condition‚Äù of your Federal Probation or Supervised Release.

Make sure there are no weapons at the new address.

STEP 4: The receiving District must approve or deny the transfer after conducting the ‚ÄúTransfer Investigation.‚Äù The receiving district can deny your request to transfer/move for any reason. The sending district where you are currently supervised must wait for a response before it can act to transfer your supervision.[799]

IMPORTANT NOTE! Some districts have hard and fast rules about certain offenses‚Äîe.g., they won‚Äôt accept people with sex offenses, or the receiving district will require a more onerous investigation of that person.
