What rights do I have during a probation revocation hearing?

There are minimal due process requirements for probation revocation proceedings.[723] This means that you don‚Äôt have all of the same rights that you have at trial.[724]

You have the following rights:

Written notice of the alleged violations and the possible consequences, with enough information to allow you to prepare a defense and obtain mitigating evidence (meaning evidence that would lessen the perceived severity of the violation or help justify it);[725]

Disclosure of the evidence against you;[726]

Timely hearing of the charges at a probable cause hearing and a formal revocation hearing;[727]

The right to present witnesses and evidence.[728] You can subpoena and present witnesses and evidence.[729] A person served with a subpoena for a parole revocation hearing is required to appear at the hearing unless the hearing is held at a place outside the county of his or her residence and more than 75 miles from his or her residence;[730]

The right to confront and cross-examine adverse witnesses.[731] You have a conditional right under the U.S. and California constitutions to confront witnesses whose statements are used against you in a probation violation hearing.[732] This means that you or your attorney may cross-examine any people who gave information or testified that you violated your probation.[733]

A fair and unbiased hearing body;[734] and

A written statement of the decision, the evidence relied on, and the reasons for revoking parole.[735]

IMPORTANT: You may waive (give up) your rights, either expressly‚Äîby saying you give up the right, or by implication‚Äîby failing to assert the right.[736] Therefore, it is important that you take advantage of your rights and complain if a right is violated.
