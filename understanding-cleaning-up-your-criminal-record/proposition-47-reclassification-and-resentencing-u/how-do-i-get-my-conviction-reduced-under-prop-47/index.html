How do I get my conviction reduced under Prop 47?

The process for getting a felony conviction reduced to a misdemeanor under Prop 47 depends on whether you are currently still serving your sentence (‚Äúunder sentence‚Äù) for the conviction, or if you have already completed your sentence.

    If you have already completed your sentence, you must ask to have your conviction ‚Äúreclassified.‚Äù (See PG.¬†1076 for information on reclassification.)
    If you are currently on formal probation for the qualifying offense, the courts are still figuring out whether you must ask for resentencing or reclassification. Some lawyers have been successful in convincing judges that you qualify for resentencing, which would could mean that you would be released from probation, so it is worthwhile to first ask to be resentenced if you‚Äôre still on formal probation.[2951]

HELPFUL HINT‚ÄîTALK TO A LAWYER! If you think you qualify for resentencing or reclassification, it is recommended that you talk to a lawyer for help. You can start with the lawyer who represented you in your case or your local Public Defender. See also the list of expungement legal aid providers on PG.¬†1180.

IMPORTANT: In general, you must apply for resentencing or reclassification before November 2017 (within 3 years of when the new law was passed), but this time limit can be waived (excused) if you have a good reason for not applying sooner.[2952]

RESENTENCING:

If you are currently serving your sentence (‚Äúunder sentence‚Äù) for an offense that qualifies under Prop 47, and you are NOT excluded by one of the disqualifying convictions, you may be eligible for RESENTENCING.[2953]

You will need to fill out and file a Petition for Resentencing, and you may need to request a hearing to convince the judge that you qualify for resentencing under Prop 47. If you qualify, the judge MUST resentence you unless s/he thinks you pose an ‚Äúunreasonable risk to public safety.‚Äù If you have already served the amount of time that you would have served if you were originally convicted of a misdemeanor, you will be released or discharged from supervision.

For information and instructions on how to petition for resentencing, including what ‚Äúunreasonable risk to public safety‚Äù means, see APPENDIX¬†T, on PG.¬†1146. Each local court has created its own version of this petition, so it‚Äôs recommended that you go to the court where you were convicted to request one.

RECLASSIFICATION:

If you already completed your sentence (including any parole or probation term) for an offense that qualifies under Prop 47, and you are NOT excluded by one of the disqualifying convictions, you may be eligible to have your felony RECLASSIFIED as a misdemeanor. Unfortunately, you cannot get back the time that you served for the felony, but you can change your criminal record to show a misdemeanor conviction instead of a felony conviction. (For information on the benefits of reducing a felony conviction to a misdemeanor, see PG.¬†1070.)

Just as for resentencing, you will need to fill out and file a Petition for Reclassification (in most courts this is the same form as for resentencing). The judge will then review your petition to make sure that you qualify for reclassification. Generally, you will not need to request a hearing for a reclassification, however, in some circumstances you may need to.

If you meet the requirements, the judge MUST reclassify your conviction as a misdemeanor.

For information and instructions on how to petition for reclassification, including when you might need to request a hearing, see APPENDIX¬†T, on PG.¬†1146.
