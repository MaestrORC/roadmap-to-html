What is my parole plan?

Your parole plan is an individualized plan to guide your parole supervision, which DAPO staff will prepare for you before your release based on an individual evaluation of your strengths, needs, and risks of reoffending. Your parole plan will include the following information: your strengths; risk of reoffending (risk level and ‚Äúcriminogenic needs‚Äù identified during your individual evaluation); goals for successful reentry (these will be related to your specific risk factors); specific ‚Äútriggers‚Äù that may lead to negative behavior (such as strategies for successful reentry and avoiding negative ‚Äútriggers‚Äù; special conditions of parole; supervision category; and other items.[455] Your supervision category is based on DAPO‚Äôs initial evaluation of your risk of reoffending, and your progress while on parole.[456]

You should receive a copy of your parole plan before you are released. While you are on parole, you should be meeting regularly with your parole agent to review your parole plan, your progress with specific goals and strategies, and other issues. Your parole agent will modify (change) your parole plan based on your progress and other changes in your situation.[457] Your supervision category should also be reviewed periodically to determine whether it should be changed.[458]

IMPORTANT: Victims and witnesses can also request special conditions, including no-contact provisions and/or that you are not paroled to the county where they live.[459] Read more about the special conditions that relate to victims and witnessed on PG.¬†178. It‚Äôs also likely that BPH or CDCR will place a no-contact provision for crime partners, preventing you from contacting/seeing anyone who was your co-defendant in the criminal case. What are examples of common special conditions of state parole?

The most common special conditions for a person on state parole are:

You cannot drink alcohol;

You must submit to drug testing; or

You must participate in mental health treatment.[461]
